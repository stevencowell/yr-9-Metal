Unit 5: Cutting & Shaping Techniques

Shearing & Punching

Shearing is a process that cuts sheet metal without creating chips or melting the material. A punch forces the work against a die with a clearance typically between 5 % and 40 % of the sheet thickness. Too little clearance increases machine wear, while too much produces large roll‑over and burrs on the cut edge. During shearing the material first plastically deforms and then fractures, resulting in a clean upper section and a rougher lower section. Punching is similar but produces a slug which can be discarded or used as a blank.

Sawing, Filing & Shaping

Hacksaws are used for cutting bars and profiles. Use steady strokes and appropriate blade pitch for the thickness of material. Bench shears or guillotines cut sheet metal quickly but require you to keep fingers clear of the blade. After cutting, remove burrs with a file; always file on the forward stroke and lift the file on the return to avoid dulling the teeth. For curves or complex shapes, use tinsnips or a jeweller’s saw, then finish with files and emery cloth.

Bending & Forming

Sheet metal can be bent using hand folders or magnetic brakes (Magnabend). Mark the bend line clearly and use a bending allowance to account for material thickness. Use a mallet and formers when hand‑bending small brackets or round bar. Forging shapes like the fishing rod holder stake involves heating the end of the rod and hammering it to a point or flattened paddle; always wear gloves when handling hot metal.
