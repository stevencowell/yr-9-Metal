Unit 3: Materials – Ferrous & Non‑Ferrous Metals

Ferrous vs Non‑Ferrous Metals

Metals fall into two broad categories: ferrous and non‑ferrous. Ferrous metals contain significant iron and are typically magnetic, dense and very strong. However, they are prone to rust if left unprotected. Examples include carbon steel, cast iron and wrought iron. Non‑ferrous metals contain little or no iron; they are lighter, generally non‑magnetic and more resistant to corrosion. Aluminium, copper, lead, zinc and tin are all non‑ferrous metals.

Ferrous metals are chosen for strength and load‑bearing applications, such as structural beams and heavy machinery frames. Cast iron is brittle but excellent for machine tool bodies because it dampens vibration. Steel is incredibly strong and can be alloyed with carbon and other elements to improve its hardness and toughness. Galvanised steel has a thin coating of zinc to prevent rusting. Non‑ferrous metals offer advantages where weight and corrosion resistance matter: aluminium is light and good for aircraft and boats, copper conducts electricity and heat well, and zinc is used for galvanising and die casting.

Heat Treatment Basics

Metals can be heat treated to change their properties. Annealing involves heating a metal above its recrystallisation temperature and then cooling it slowly to relieve internal stresses, increase ductility and reduce hardness. Quenching rapidly cools a metal, usually steel, from a high temperature to produce a hard but brittle martensitic structure; the cooling media can vary from brine to oil. Tempering reheats a quenched metal to a lower temperature (e.g. 205–595 °C) to reduce brittleness and improve toughness. When selecting materials for a project, consider both the base properties and how heat treatment can modify them.

Selecting Materials

In metalwork you may use sheet, bar, tube or solid stock. Choose materials based on the product’s purpose: sheet aluminium for a lightweight panel, steel bar for a strong bracket or brass rod for decorative appeal. Consider whether the metal will be exposed to the elements and if it requires a protective coating or heat treatment. Understanding the properties of ferrous and non‑ferrous metals helps you make informed decisions when designing and fabricating your projects.
