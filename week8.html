Unit 8: Heat Treatment & Finishing

Heat Treatment Processes

Heat treatment alters a metal’s microstructure to change its properties. In annealing, the metal is heated above its recrystallisation temperature, held there and then cooled slowly. This increases ductility and reduces hardness. Quenching involves rapidly cooling a metal, typically from above the upper critical temperature. For steel, this creates a very hard martensitic structure but also makes it brittle; quench media range from brine (fastest) to oil or air (slowest). After quenching, tempering reheats the steel to a moderate temperature (around 205–595 °C) and then cools it again to relieve brittleness and impart toughness.

Surface Preparation & Finishing

A good finish begins with preparation. Remove scratches and tooling marks by filing and sanding through progressively finer grits. Clean the surface of grease and dust. Buffing uses a cloth wheel and abrasive compound to produce a mirror sheen; polishing uses finer compounds for an even brighter finish. For protective finishes, painting and powder coating cover the metal in a coloured film. Powder coating applies a dry polymer powder electrostatically and then cures it in an oven, resulting in a durable, even finish. Plastic coating (such as dipping in plastisol) can provide a thick protective layer. Always follow manufacturer instructions and allow coatings to cure fully.
