Unit 6: Joining Techniques

Mechanical Fastening

Mechanical fasteners join metals without melting them. Common fasteners include screws, bolts, nuts and rivets. Screws and bolts create a clamping force and can be removed for maintenance. Rivets are permanent: pop rivets (blind rivets) are installed from one side using a rivet gun, while solid rivets require access to both sides and are set using a hammer and dolly. Spacing and edge distance are important to avoid tearing the sheet.

Soldering & Brazing

Soldering uses a low‑melting filler metal (typically tin‑based) to join parts without melting the base metals. It’s ideal for delicate work like electronics or thin sheet metal seams. Brazing is similar but uses a higher‑melting filler (often brass) to create a stronger joint. Both processes require clean surfaces and flux to promote bonding. Heat the joint evenly until the filler flows, then allow it to cool without disturbance.

Welding

Welding melts the base metals together to form the strongest joints. In gas metal arc welding (GMAW, also known as MIG), an electric arc forms between a consumable wire electrode and the workpiece, heating the metals and causing them to fuse. A shielding gas (often argon/CO₂ mix) protects the molten metal from atmospheric contamination. Always wear appropriate PPE: a welding helmet, gloves and protective clothing.

Tapping & Threads

Internal threads are cut using taps; external threads are formed with dies. When tapping a hole, select the correct size drill (tap drill) and use cutting fluid to lubricate the tap. Rotate the tap a half‑turn forward and quarter‑turn back to break chips; this prevents the tap from binding and breaking. Remove swarf frequently.
